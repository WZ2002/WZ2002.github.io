Climate Change as a Non-Traditional Threat in Global Security

Apr 29, 2024 Yunzhi Zhang Security Studies

When we think of global security threats, images of war, terrorism, or cyberattacks often come to mind. Yet, the 21st century has brought a sobering realization: some of the most profound dangers to human survival are not armed conflicts but the silent, insidious challenges we have labeled as "non-traditional threats." Among these, climate change stands out—not just as an environmental issue but as a force reshaping global security. To understand its impact, we must explore not only its current implications but also draw critical lessons from history.

Unlike traditional threats, which are typically caused by deliberate human actions, climate change emerges from the collective, cumulative behavior of societies over centuries. Rising sea levels, extreme weather events, and resource scarcity are no longer future predictions but present realities. These phenomena destabilize nations, exacerbate inequalities, and even ignite conflicts. Consider the Syrian Civil War, where prolonged drought played a significant role in displacing rural populations and fueling unrest—a potent reminder that climate stress can amplify societal tensions.

However, climate change's true threat lies in its unpredictability and interconnectedness. It does not recognize borders, nor can it be subdued by a ceasefire. Instead, it challenges the foundational structures of global security: food systems, water availability, and even geopolitical stability.

History offers ample examples of societies unraveling in the face of environmental shifts. One of the most famous cases is the collapse of the Mayan civilization. For centuries, the Maya thrived, building sophisticated cities and cultural systems. Yet, prolonged droughts, exacerbated by deforestation and overpopulation, led to food shortages, internal conflicts, and eventual societal decline.

Similarly, the Little Ice Age (roughly 1300–1850) caused famines, economic downturns, and political instability across Europe and Asia. The Ottoman Empire, for instance, struggled with agricultural shortfalls and rebellions, while Europe saw its own upheavals, including the Thirty Years' War—a conflict deeply influenced by resource scarcity and climate-induced pressures.

These historical precedents reveal a critical truth: environmental stress can act as a catalyst, transforming latent vulnerabilities into crises. The fall of civilizations is rarely due to a single cause but often the interplay of environmental, social, and political factors.

So, how can we apply these lessons to today's challenges? First, history underscores the importance of resilience. Societies that adapt creatively to environmental changes—like the Netherlands with its water management systems—are better equipped to weather crises. By contrast, those that ignore or exacerbate environmental problems risk collapse.

Second, historical case studies remind us that climate change is not a standalone issue but a multiplier. It magnifies preexisting inequalities and conflicts, disproportionately affecting vulnerable populations. This requires global policies to address not only environmental concerns but also their social and economic dimensions.

Lastly, history shows the perils of delay. The Maya, the Ottomans, and other societies likely recognized their environmental problems but failed to act in time. Modern governments must avoid this trap by investing in proactive, long-term solutions rather than reactive measures.

Addressing climate change as a security issue demands a paradigm shift. Traditional security frameworks prioritize immediate threats, such as military aggression, but often overlook slow-moving dangers like environmental degradation. This must change. Nations and international bodies need to integrate climate considerations into their security strategies, recognizing that a flooded city or a decimated crop yield can be as destabilizing as any war.

Innovative approaches like climate diplomacy, green technology investments, and multilateral cooperation are essential. For example, the Paris Agreement, while imperfect, represents a step toward treating climate change as a collective challenge requiring global solidarity.

Climate change is rewriting the rules of global security, challenging us to think beyond conventional threats and embrace a more holistic perspective. By studying history, we gain a clearer understanding of the stakes and the consequences of inaction.

But history also offers hope. Societies that learn from their mistakes and adapt to new realities often emerge stronger. The question is whether we will heed these lessons or repeat the errors of the past. In an era where every fraction of a degree matters, the time to act is not tomorrow, but today.

The fight against climate change is not just an environmental battle; it is a struggle for the security and stability of humanity itself. History tells us what happens when we ignore such challenges. Let us ensure the future tells a different story.

Border Cultures and Conflicts in Travel

May 13, 2024 Yunzhi Zhang Travel

Borders, by their very nature, are paradoxical. On one hand, they delineate order—defining nations, cultures, and systems of governance. On the other, they embody tension, acting as sites of negotiation, conflict, and identity. For travelers, borders are more than lines on a map; they are thresholds where security, culture, and personal experience collide. This collision can be enlightening, but it also reveals deeper truths about our world’s divisions and the way we perceive "the other."

Traveling through borders is not merely a logistical necessity; it is an intimate confrontation with the human-made boundaries that separate us. What do these encounters tell us about global security, cultural exchange, and our shared humanity?

In the modern world, borders are the physical manifestation of national security. They are policed and fortified to protect against perceived threats—whether it be illegal immigration, smuggling, or even pandemics. For governments, borders are lines of control; for travelers, they are points of vulnerability. A missing document, an overzealous officer, or a misunderstood rule can suddenly turn the crossing into an exercise in anxiety.

But borders are more than just barriers; they are also mirrors. They reflect the fears, priorities, and prejudices of the nations they divide. The heavily militarized demarcation between North and South Korea speaks to decades of unresolved conflict. Meanwhile, the open borders within the European Union highlight a vision of unity and shared prosperity. For travelers, these contrasts are often stark reminders of the differing ways nations define safety and belonging.

Borders are not just political constructs—they are cultural frontiers. Often, they are places where two or more ways of life meet, mix, and sometimes clash. For the curious traveler, this can be fascinating. The markets in Tijuana, Mexico, just a stone’s throw from San Diego, showcase a vibrant blend of American and Mexican influences. Similarly, the towns along the Silk Road in Central Asia remain cultural mosaics, bearing traces of the countless traders and travelers who crossed them centuries ago.

Yet, borders can also magnify differences. Crossing from India into Pakistan, one is struck not only by the physical checkpoints but also by the emotional weight of history. Partition, war, and decades of animosity linger in the air. The famed daily border ceremony at Wagah, with its choreographed aggression and flag-lowering rituals, is equal parts spectacle and metaphor—a reminder of how borders can simultaneously separate and unite.

Not all border experiences are as theatrical. In some regions, borders are zones of active conflict, where questions of security and identity are violently contested. The ongoing disputes in places like Israel and Palestine or the Kashmir region illustrate how borders can become flashpoints, with dire consequences for those who live near or travel through them.

Even travelers, often seen as neutral parties, are not immune to these tensions. These moments are humbling, serving as a stark reminder that the freedom to cross borders is a privilege not shared by all.

For travelers, borders are often spaces of storytelling. Each crossing comes with its own narrative—of unexpected kindness, bureaucratic frustration, or cultural revelation. These stories shape how we understand the world and our place in it.

However, it is also important to reflect critically on our role as travelers. Tourism is not a neutral act; it is shaped by the very inequalities that borders enforce. While many of us move freely with powerful passports, millions of people are trapped by borders, their mobility restricted by economic, political, or humanitarian crises.

How can travelers engage with borders responsibly? By being conscious of privilege, by listening to local perspectives, and by understanding the historical and geopolitical contexts of the places we visit.

In an era of globalization, some dream of a borderless world—a utopia where people, goods, and ideas flow freely. Yet, history cautions against oversimplification. Borders, for all their flaws, also protect. They safeguard cultural identities and offer refuge to those fleeing persecution. The challenge is not to erase borders but to rethink them: to make them less about exclusion and more about connection.

Traveling through borders is more than a physical act; it is an intellectual and emotional journey. Each crossing forces us to confront questions of identity, security, and belonging. Are borders necessary, or are they artificial constructs that divide us unnecessarily? Can we create a world where crossing a border feels less like an interrogation and more like an invitation?

The answer lies not only in how governments manage borders but also in how we, as individuals, approach them. By treating borders as spaces of learning rather than barriers, we can challenge their divisive nature. The next time you find yourself at a border—whether it’s a bustling airport or a remote mountain pass—pause for a moment. Consider what that boundary represents, not just for the nations it separates but for the human stories it holds.

In exploring the boundaries of security, we are ultimately exploring the boundaries of our own understanding. And perhaps, in doing so, we can learn to bridge the divides that borders so often reinforce.

How the Legacy of Empires Shapes Global Security through an Internationalist Lens

June 6, 2024 Yunzhi Zhang Security Studies

Empires are not merely relics of history; their legacies remain embedded in the frameworks of modern international relations. While the imperial era has ostensibly passed, the borders, systems, and power dynamics they created continue to influence global security. From enduring conflicts rooted in colonial boundary-making to the emergence of neo-imperial forms of influence, the legacy of empires cannot be understood in isolation. However, by adopting an internationalist perspective, we can move beyond the zero-sum logic of power and work toward a more equitable and cooperative global order.

Few artifacts of imperial rule have left as significant an imprint on global security as borders. Often drawn arbitrarily by colonial powers, these lines have divided communities, imposed artificial nation-states, and sown the seeds of long-lasting tensions.

For example, in Africa, the Berlin Conference of 1884-85 allowed European powers to carve up the continent with little regard for ethnic, cultural, or geographical realities. The result was a patchwork of nation-states that forced rival groups into shared political systems or divided cohesive communities across multiple borders. These decisions, made without African voices at the table, have perpetuated conflicts, from the Nigerian Civil War to ongoing tensions in the Great Lakes region.

Similarly, in the Middle East, the Sykes-Picot Agreement of 1916, crafted by British and French diplomats, redrew the map of the region with little regard for its peoples. The consequences—persistent unrest in Iraq, Syria, and Palestine—are stark reminders of the dangers of unilateral decision-making. The borders imposed during these imperial ventures have become fault lines for some of the world’s most intractable conflicts.

From an internationalist perspective, these examples underline the need for participatory approaches in addressing border conflicts. The principle of self-determination—enshrined in the UN Charter—must be central to any resolution of disputes rooted in colonial legacies. International cooperation, rather than domination, is the path to lasting peace.

Though traditional empires have dissolved, their structures persist in the form of neo-imperialism. In today’s world, imperial power is no longer exerted through territorial conquest but through economic dominance, political interventions, and cultural hegemony.

The United States, often described as the archetype of a modern empire, wields its influence through a vast network of military bases, global financial institutions, and multinational corporations. Meanwhile, China’s Belt and Road Initiative, with its extensive investments in infrastructure across Asia, Africa, and Europe, reflects a new form of imperial ambition—one that critics argue creates economic dependencies disguised as development aid.

However, an internationalist framework challenges the competitive logic of neo-imperialism. Rather than focusing on unilateral gains, internationalism calls for multilateral institutions to act as mediators and facilitators of equitable development. The question is not which power dominates the world order but how nations can collectively address shared challenges such as climate change, global inequality, and pandemics.

Empires did not just redraw maps; they also reshaped cultural and intellectual landscapes. The spread of languages like English, French, and Spanish reflects the enduring influence of imperial rule. Today, these languages dominate international diplomacy, trade, and education, often at the expense of indigenous and local languages.

Similarly, Western legal and political systems, introduced during colonial rule, remain foundational in many countries. While these systems have facilitated modernization in some contexts, they have also marginalized traditional governance structures and ways of life. For example, indigenous land rights movements around the world—from Canada to Australia—highlight the tension between colonial legal frameworks and indigenous sovereignty.

An internationalist approach to cultural legacies emphasizes inclusivity and mutual respect. This involves not only recognizing the value of diverse traditions but also addressing historical injustices. Initiatives like UNESCO’s efforts to preserve endangered languages and cultures are examples of how international cooperation can counteract the homogenizing tendencies of imperial legacies.

The legacy of empires intersects with today’s geopolitical shifts as we move toward a multipolar world. Rising powers like China, India, and Brazil are challenging the dominance of traditional Western powers, creating opportunities for a more balanced global order. However, this transition must not replicate the competitive, imperial dynamics of the past.

Internationalism offers a framework for navigating this transformation. Instead of perpetuating power struggles, it calls for collaboration through institutions like the United Nations, regional organizations, and global civil society networks. Efforts to reform international institutions—to make them more representative and equitable—are essential to ensuring that emerging powers contribute to a cooperative, rather than divisive, world order.

Empires remind us of the dangers of unchecked power, but they also highlight humanity’s capacity for connection and exchange. If we learn from history and act collectively, the legacy of empires can become not a source of division, but a foundation for global solidarity.

Cities Where History Meets Modernity: Reflections from Rome to Kyoto

Aug 20, 2024 Yunzhi Zhang Travel

Cities are places where the old and the new coexist, sometimes in harmony, sometimes in tension. Among them, Rome and Kyoto stand out as luminous examples of how history and modernity intertwine, creating urban spaces that are both timeless and dynamic. While their contexts differ—one a European powerhouse shaped by empires, the other a Japanese cultural capital shaped by centuries of tradition—these cities share a unique ability to straddle eras. To walk their streets is to step into an open dialogue between past and present.

Rome, famously dubbed the Eternal City, wears its history like a crown. From the monumental ruins of the Colosseum to the grandeur of St. Peter’s Basilica, the city pulsates with the echoes of its imperial and religious past. Yet, what strikes a modern visitor most is not just Rome’s ancient glory but how seamlessly it integrates its history into daily life.

Strolling through the Roman Forum, one marvels at the architectural achievements of antiquity, only to emerge onto a bustling piazza filled with Vespa scooters and espresso-drinking locals. Rome’s layered urban fabric is a testament to its resilience: medieval churches are built atop Roman temples, Renaissance palaces abut modern office buildings, and even ancient aqueducts still deliver water to the city. History here is not a static relic but a lived experience—a backdrop to contemporary life.

But Rome is also a city of paradoxes. Its attempts to modernize, from Mussolini’s fascist-era urban planning to contemporary infrastructure projects, have often clashed with its historical character. The debate over how to preserve Rome’s heritage while embracing modernity is ongoing, reflecting broader global questions about urban development and cultural identity.

In contrast to Rome’s grandeur, Kyoto’s charm lies in its understated elegance. Once the imperial capital of Japan, Kyoto is a city that has managed to retain its historical soul despite the rapid modernization of Japan as a whole. With its meticulously preserved temples, tea houses, and traditional wooden machiya townhouses, Kyoto feels like a city where time flows differently.

Yet Kyoto, too, is a place of contrasts. The ancient ritual of tea ceremonies exists alongside bustling shopping streets filled with trendy boutiques. The UNESCO-listed Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion) gleams with timeless beauty, but a short train ride away, you find Kyoto Station—a sleek, futuristic hub of glass and steel that symbolizes Japan’s forward-looking ethos.

Kyoto’s ability to maintain its cultural continuity is partly a result of deliberate policies. Unlike other Japanese cities that were heavily bombed during World War II, Kyoto’s historical architecture was spared, allowing it to preserve its heritage. Additionally, local ordinances restrict building heights and mandate the use of traditional aesthetics in certain districts. The Intersection of Global Security and Historical Inquiry: How the Past Shapes the Future

Oct 19, 2024 Yunzhi Zhang Security Studies

Global security is often framed as a forward-looking field, preoccupied with emerging threats, technological advancements, and strategies for maintaining peace in an unpredictable world. History, on the other hand, is often seen as the study of what has already happened—a reflection on events that no longer hold sway over the present. Yet, to view them as separate disciplines is to miss a crucial truth: the past and the future are inextricably linked.

The study of history is not just about understanding what happened; it’s about uncovering the patterns, decisions, and dynamics that continue to shape our world. Global security, in turn, cannot be effectively pursued without an appreciation of the historical forces that underlie contemporary conflicts and alliances. The past is not a relic—it is a roadmap, a resource, and a warning.

Many of today’s global security challenges are rooted in historical grievances, unresolved conflicts, and colonial legacies. To address these issues without understanding their origins is akin to treating symptoms without diagnosing the underlying disease.

Take the Middle East, for example. The conflicts in Iraq, Syria, and Palestine are often discussed in terms of religious extremism, geopolitical rivalry, or resource competition. But these crises cannot be fully understood without acknowledging the role of European colonialism in the early 20th century. The Sykes-Picot Agreement of 1916 divided the region into arbitrary spheres of influence, ignoring cultural, tribal, and religious dynamics. This act sowed seeds of distrust and instability that continue to shape the region’s geopolitics.

Similarly, the ethnic tensions that have fueled conflicts in Rwanda, the Balkans, and Myanmar have historical roots in colonial practices of divide-and-rule or in long-standing grievances that were never resolved. By studying these histories, policymakers and security experts can better anticipate flashpoints and design strategies that address not only the symptoms of conflict but also their root causes.

History is replete with lessons about power dynamics, diplomacy, and the consequences of short-term thinking. One of the clearest examples is the Treaty of Versailles, which ended World War I. While it sought to secure peace, its punitive terms against Germany laid the groundwork for resentment and economic hardship, ultimately contributing to the rise of Adolf Hitler and the outbreak of World War II. This serves as a cautionary tale for how peace agreements, if not carefully designed, can inadvertently set the stage for future conflicts.

On the other hand, history also offers examples of successful conflict resolution. The Marshall Plan, implemented after World War II, not only rebuilt Europe’s economy but also fostered long-term alliances and stability. By investing in the reconstruction of war-torn nations, the United States recognized that security was not just about military strength but also about economic resilience and political collaboration.

The Cold War provides another rich source of historical lessons. The strategies of containment, deterrence, and arms control agreements like the INF Treaty demonstrate how rival powers can manage tensions without descending into open conflict. These lessons are particularly relevant in today’s context of rising great-power competition between the United States and China, as well as the resurgence of Russia as a disruptive force on the global stage.

Failing to account for history in global security decisions often leads to disastrous outcomes. The U.S.-led invasion of Iraq in 2003 is a stark example. The decision to dismantle Iraq’s Ba’athist government without understanding the country’s sectarian complexities created a power vacuum that was quickly filled by extremist groups like ISIS. This miscalculation was, in part, a failure to consider Iraq’s modern history, including its fragile balance of Sunni, Shia, and Kurdish populations.

Similarly, interventions in Afghanistan, Libya, and other conflict zones often suffer from a lack of historical awareness. Policies are designed based on immediate objectives—defeating an insurgency, toppling a regime—without a long-term understanding of how local histories, tribal politics, and societal structures will react to foreign interference. The result is often instability that outlasts the intervention itself.

While history cannot predict the future with precision, it can illuminate patterns and trajectories. For instance, the historical cycles of rising and falling great powers offer insights into today’s shifting global order. The Thucydides Trap—a concept derived from the ancient historian’s account of the Peloponnesian War—warns of the potential for conflict when a rising power threatens an established one. This framework is frequently invoked to analyze the U.S.-China rivalry, helping policymakers anticipate areas of tension and opportunities for diplomacy.

In the realm of cybersecurity, history also provides valuable lessons. Just as nations during the Industrial Revolution struggled to adapt to the rapid pace of technological change, today’s leaders face the challenge of managing the risks of artificial intelligence, quantum computing, and cyberwarfare. By studying historical transitions—whether in technology, governance, or warfare—we can better prepare for the challenges of our own era.

Beyond strategy and policy, history reminds us of the human dimension of global security. It tells the stories of those who have lived through wars, migrations, and upheavals, offering a perspective that numbers and statistics cannot capture. This human element is essential for crafting policies that are not only effective but also just.

For example, historical accounts of the civilian experience during World War II—whether in Hiroshima, Dresden, or Nanjing—underscore the importance of protecting non-combatants in modern warfare. These stories challenge us to think critically about the ethical dimensions of security, from drone strikes to refugee policies.

The intersection of history and global security is not just about learning from the past—it’s about using that knowledge to shape a more stable and equitable future. This requires a mindset that values historical context as much as data analytics and military strategy. It means bringing historians into conversations about security policy, ensuring that decisions are informed by a deep understanding of how we got here.

It also requires humility. History shows us that even the most powerful empires and institutions are not immune to decline. It reminds us that security is not a zero-sum game but a shared endeavor, one that depends on cooperation, resilience, and a long-term vision.

As we navigate the complexities of global security in the 21st century, history remains one of our most valuable tools. It provides context for understanding today’s challenges, warns against the pitfalls of short-term thinking, and offers inspiration for crafting solutions that endure.

The past may not hold all the answers, but it does hold the questions we must ask if we are to build a future that is not only secure but also just. The intersection of history and global security is not just academic—it is deeply practical. Because the future, as uncertain as it may be, will always be shaped by the echoes of the past.

And if we listen closely, those echoes might just guide us toward a better world.

The Crusades, for example, were not merely wars over territory but also clashes between two distinct religious and cultural worldviews—Christian Europe and the Islamic world. Not all cross-cultural interactions have been violent. The Silk Road, which connected the East and West, is often celebrated as a symbol of cultural exchange and mutual enrichment. However, even this vibrant network of trade was not free from conflict. Disputes over trade routes, competition between empires, and the spread of diseases like the Black Death revealed the fragility of such connections.

These examples highlight a critical point: while cultural exchange has the potential to foster understanding, it also carries risks, particularly when economic or political interests are at stake.

Fast forward to today, and globalization has amplified both the opportunities for cultural understanding and the risks of cross-cultural conflict. The internet, international trade, and mass migration have brought people closer together than ever before. Yet, they have also exposed fault lines that history left behind.

One of the paradoxes of globalization is that it promotes cultural homogenization while simultaneously provoking resistance to it. The spread of Western brands, entertainment, and values is often viewed as a threat to local traditions and identities. This has led to pushback in the form of nationalism and cultural revival movements.

For example, the dominance of English in global communication is both a unifying force and a source of tension. While it facilitates international collaboration, it also marginalizes non-English-speaking cultures, creating a sense of cultural erosion. This tension is evident in countries like France, where efforts to protect the French language from the encroachment of English are as much about preserving national identity as they are about linguistics.

Mass migration, whether driven by conflict, climate change, or economic opportunity, is another flashpoint of modern cross-cultural conflict. Host countries often struggle to balance integration with the preservation of social cohesion, while migrants face the challenge of adapting to new environments without losing their cultural roots.

The rise of anti-immigration sentiment in many Western nations reflects deep anxieties about cultural change. These fears are not new—they echo historical examples of scapegoating and exclusion—but they are now amplified by the speed and scale of global migration.

Technology is often hailed as a bridge between cultures, yet it can just as easily become a tool for division. Social media platforms, for instance, enable the rapid spread of misinformation and stereotypes, fueling misunderstandings and prejudices. At the same time, algorithms that reinforce echo chambers can deepen cultural divides rather than bridging them.

If history teaches us anything, it is that cross-cultural conflicts are not inevitable. They arise when differences are weaponized for political or economic gain, or when opportunities for dialogue are ignored. So how can we apply these lessons to navigate the challenges of modern globalization?

First, education and historical awareness are critical tools in addressing cross-cultural conflicts. Understanding the historical roots of these tensions allows us to approach them with empathy and informed perspectives. Education systems should prioritize teaching global history in a way that emphasizes interconnectedness and shared humanity rather than division. For example, acknowledging colonial histories and their ongoing impacts helps societies come to terms with the past and provides the foundation for reconciliation. A curriculum that explores the complexities of cultural exchanges, rather than presenting simplified narratives of conflict, can foster a more nuanced understanding of the world.

Second, the process of globalization must be made more inclusive. For globalization to succeed without exacerbating cultural tensions, it needs to respect and value diverse cultural perspectives rather than subsuming them under a single dominant narrative. This includes amplifying marginalized voices in global institutions such as the United Nations and creating equitable opportunities for participation in international decision-making. Inclusivity can also extend to economic systems, ensuring that globalization’s benefits are distributed fairly and not concentrated among wealthier nations at the expense of historically exploited ones.

Another key lesson from history is the importance of creating platforms for dialogue. Many cross-cultural conflicts stem from a lack of understanding, and fostering opportunities for dialogue can build bridges rather than deepen divides. These platforms can operate at multiple levels—local, national, or international. For example, grassroots community initiatives can promote mutual understanding, while international cultural programs such as UNESCO’s World Heritage initiatives demonstrate how celebrating cultural diversity can strengthen global security. Dialogue allows people to confront their biases, find common ground, and humanize those who might otherwise seem distant or unfamiliar.

Finally, we must recognize the dual role of technology in cross-cultural interactions. While technology has often been a tool for division, it also holds immense potential for fostering connection. When used thoughtfully, digital platforms can enable people to collaborate and share experiences across borders, breaking down stereotypes and building empathy. Virtual exchanges between schools, cross-cultural online forums, and collaborative global projects are just a few examples of how technology can facilitate understanding. However, this requires intentional design and governance to prevent misinformation and the creation of divisive echo chambers.

By learning from history and adopting these strategies, we can tackle the challenges of globalization and cross-cultural conflict. The past provides us with valuable lessons—not only about the mistakes we must avoid but also about the possibilities for a future built on mutual respect and cooperation.
