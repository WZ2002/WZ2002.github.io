What Oil's Market Teaches Us About Renewables

Yunzhi Zhang · Sep 6, 2025 · 10 min read

The energy transition is not only a matter of policy and technology; it is also fundamentally an economic question. Understanding the economic value of oil, the most important energy source for humanity over the past century, helps us see more clearly the pathways and challenges of moving toward new energy systems.

Daniel Yergin's The Prize provides a powerful account of the history and economics of oil. Chapter 36 in particular offers a vivid case study of how oil's economic value became tied to market forces and political decisions, and how oil, still the strongest competitor to renewable energy, was shaped by global bargaining, policy shifts, and structural changes in the industry.

Oil as a "Global Bathtub" Market

One of the simplest but most important logics of oil economics is the so-called "bathtub" analogy. All oil produced worldwide, regardless of where it comes from, flows into a single metaphorical bathtub. Likewise, consumers everywhere draw oil out of the same bathtub. There are no isolated oil markets. Crude oil is highly substitutable: no matter where it is produced, refiners can process it into similar products used worldwide.

OPEC, the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries, currently produces around 30–35% of the world's oil supply and has historically exerted outsized influence on price by coordinating production levels. Within the bathtub framework, the logic is simple: if production falls, global supply tightens and prices rise; if production increases, prices fall.

OPEC's Quotas and the Prisoner's Dilemma

For decades, OPEC sought to stabilize prices and profits through production quotas negotiated at member meetings. In theory, coordination would maximize collective benefits. In practice, however, OPEC members often behaved like players in an endless prisoner's dilemma. While cooperation promised higher and more stable revenues, the temptation to "cheat" by secretly pumping above quota was strong. Cheating provided short-term gain but eroded trust and pushed prices downward, creating instability in the long run.

Adding to OPEC's difficulty was the rise of non-OPEC producers, notably North Sea oil (UK and Norway), Alaskan fields, and other new regions, as well as competition from alternative energy sources such as coal, natural gas, and nuclear power. These weakened OPEC's share of global supply and its ability to enforce discipline.

Deregulation in the UK: The End of BNOC

In the 1970s, the UK established the British National Oil Company (BNOC) to market North Sea oil and play a role in price-setting. But as prices softened in the early 1980s, BNOC began losing money, buying oil high and selling it low. Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher, a strong believer in free markets, abolished BNOC in 1985. UK oil would no longer be sold through a state intermediary but directly into the open market. OPEC thus lost a significant external price-support mechanism, and oil markets became even more commoditized, with prices determined by thousands of private transactions rather than government-to-government deals.

Saudi Arabia's Turning Point

Saudi Arabia, OPEC's largest and most influential producer, was hit hardest. The kingdom alone accounts for about one-third of OPEC's production, and for years it acted as the "swing producer," adjusting its own output to stabilize prices. But by 1985 this strategy had become ruinous. Saudi revenues fell from $119 billion in 1981 to just $26 billion in 1985, forcing budget cuts and draining reserves. Meanwhile, its own output had dropped below that of the UK North Sea.

Saudi Arabia repeatedly warned that if others continued to cheat, it would "open the floodgates", that is, abandon restraint and flood the market with crude, driving down prices for everyone. By late 1985, Riyadh changed course. It introduced netback contracts, selling crude linked to refining margins rather than fixed official prices. This effectively relinquished its role as price stabilizer and let the market set prices.

The result was dramatic: in 1986, oil prices collapsed from $31 per barrel to around $10, with Persian Gulf crude briefly hitting $6. Consumers benefited, but producers everywhere suffered. U.S. independent oil companies in Texas went bankrupt in large numbers, and even Saudi Arabia faced severe fiscal stress.

The U.S. Response and the Return to Quotas

The Reagan administration split between free-market advocates and those worried about the collapse of the domestic oil sector. Vice President George H. W. Bush, drawing on his personal experience in the oil industry, traveled to Saudi Arabia and hinted that if prices remained too low, the U.S. might impose import tariffs, a prospect that risked damaging the U.S.–Saudi strategic relationship.

Saudi Arabia's earlier decision to flood the market was not meant as a long-term strategy: while it served to punish cheaters and reassert discipline within OPEC, persistently low prices were also harmful to the kingdom's own fiscal stability.

By late 1986, OPEC members, including even Iran, then at war with Iraq, accepted the need to restore quotas. A new consensus emerged around an $18 target price, and markets stabilized in the $15–18 range.

Lessons: Oil as a Truly Global Market

The price swings of the 1970s and 1980s demonstrated that oil truly operates as a global bathtub market. Supply decisions anywhere affect prices everywhere. Producers, consumers, and governments alike learned that supply discipline and demand resilience were equally important in determining oil's value.

This period also reshaped consumer countries' strategies. The International Energy Agency (IEA) was established in 1974 to coordinate the response of major importers to supply shocks. The U.S. created the Energy Information Administration (EIA) in 1977 and built the Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR) as both a national-security asset and a tool to counterbalance OPEC's influence. In effect, consumers carved out their own leverage in the supposedly producer-dominated bathtub.

Critical Reflection: Oil vs. the New Energy Transition

The story of oil's commoditization offers lessons for today's energy transition. Oil proved resilient because it is fungible, globally traded, and deeply embedded in economic systems. Even when prices collapsed, producers kept pumping because shutting down wells was costly, and demand remained strong. Renewables face the opposite challenge: while their marginal cost of generation is near zero, their value depends on infrastructure (grids, storage, transmission) and on policies that integrate them into markets.

For renewables to play the same global role that oil once did, two things need to happen.

First, cheap electricity from wind and solar has to be given a meaningful market value. The marginal cost of renewable generation is close to zero, but its system value depends on when and where it can be delivered. Markets therefore need more price signals that reflect time and location, for example, more granular time-of-use pricing, compensation for flexibility and ancillary services, and products that value capacity and availability.

Second, regions rich in renewable resources must be able to share them across borders. Just as oil from the Middle East could be shipped worldwide, clean power needs channels to move from where it is cheapest to where it is needed. This can be done either by building high-voltage transmission lines to send renewable electricity across countries, or by converting surplus renewable power into hydrogen, ammonia, or other fuels that can be traded internationally.

In other words, building wind farms and solar parks is only the first step, the real challenge is creating the networks and trade systems that make renewable energy a reliable global commodity.
