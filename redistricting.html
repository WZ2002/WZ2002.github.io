Redistricting and Gerrymandering: the Midterm Map Wars

Yunzhi Zhang · Aug 20, 2025 · 10 min read

After weeks of legislative maneuvering and protests, the Texas House approved a new congressional map on August 20, 2025, by a decisive 88–52 vote. Gerrymandering, the manipulation of electoral district boundaries for partisan gain, has once again moved to the center of American politics. So how does redistricting shape the partisan balance of the House?

How Seats Are Decided

In the Senate, each state has two seats regardless of population. The House of Representatives' 435 seats, by contrast, are apportioned among the states based on population figures collected every ten years through the U.S. Census. This decennial apportionment dictates how many congressional districts each state will have, and states then redraw their internal district boundaries to ensure each district has roughly equal population. Because state legislatures oversee this redistricting process, it presents an opportunity for gerrymandering, the strategic manipulation of district lines for partisan advantage.

In most states, including Texas, redistricting authority lies with the legislature and requires approval by both chambers. A growing number of states, however, have shifted that power to independent or bipartisan commissions in an effort to promote fairness and reduce partisan manipulation.

Texas: A Strategic Push to Cement GOP Control

In July 2025, Democratic members of Congress from Texas held a press conference to address the state's upcoming redistricting reforms, driven by the Republican-controlled legislature. Although the most recent redistricting had followed the 2020 census, Texas lawmakers initiated an atypical mid-decade redraw in mid-2025 designed to reshape the partisan balance in Congress.

President Donald Trump reportedly pressured state legislators to act ahead of the 2026 elections, seeking to strengthen the party's narrow House majority. By creating a cushion of additional safe Republican districts, Governor Greg Abbott and the GOP-led legislature aimed to transform the party's fragile five-seat majority (220 seats) into a more durable 15-seat advantage (225 seats). If successful, the maneuver could alter the dynamics of the 2026 midterm elections and significantly raise the threshold for Democrats to reclaim control.

Democrats in Texas strongly opposed the move, with dozens of lawmakers even temporarily fleeing the state in protest. Despite the walkout, the Senate pushed ahead and approved its version of the map by a 19–2 vote in the absence of many Democrats. On August 20, the House passed its own version 88–52; after one final procedural vote, the measure returns to the Senate for concurrence before moving to Governor Abbott's desk.

Other Republican-led states, such as Ohio, Missouri, Indiana, and Florida, are reportedly weighing similar mid-decade efforts. Collectively, these moves could further entrench partisan advantages in ways that extend well beyond the next election cycle.

Notably, four of Texas's newly proposed districts would be majority-Hispanic. While Republicans made historic gains among Latino voters in 2020, the strategy carries risk: shifts in policy priorities or voter sentiment could erode those advantages over time.

California: A Partisan Counteroffensive

In response, Democrats in California are exploring a dramatic countermove. Governor Gavin Newsom and legislative leaders have proposed suspending the authority of the state's independent redistricting commission, established by voters in 2010, and replacing it with a legislature-drawn map designed to favor Democrats.

The plan could add as many as five Democratic-leaning seats to the state's already lopsided congressional delegation (currently 40 of 52 seats). Supporters have framed the proposal as a necessary "defense of democracy," arguing it would counterbalance partisan manipulation in Texas and other GOP-led states.

To implement the shift, Democrats are drafting new maps and preparing a statewide referendum for November 4, 2025. Because the proposal would overturn a voter-approved system, it must itself be ratified by voters. If enacted, the legislature's map would remain in effect until the 2030 census cycle.

A Redistricting Arms Race

Taken together, developments in Texas and California illustrate how redistricting has become a partisan arms race. As states deploy increasingly aggressive tactics to secure advantage, the risks extend beyond short-term partisan gains.

When electoral outcomes are effectively predetermined by map-drawing, public trust in the democratic process erodes. Voters in heavily gerrymandered states may feel their ballots carry little weight or that results are manipulated. This perception can undermine the legitimacy of governing institutions and deepen political polarization.

The absence of uniform national standards exacerbates the problem. Some states rely on independent commissions or prioritize neutral criteria such as compactness and community integrity, while others pursue overtly partisan maps. Without consistent guardrails, whichever party controls key state legislatures can effectively lock in structural advantages in Congress, inviting a cycle of legal battles and escalating retaliation.

Policy Responses to Break the Cycle

To prevent a permanent redistricting arms race, several measures merit consideration:

  1. Independent commissions. States should be encouraged, or required, to adopt independent or bipartisan commissions for map-drawing. Evidence from states such as Arizona and Colorado suggests these commissions produce more competitive districts and fairer outcomes. Expanding their adoption through legislation or ballot initiatives could mitigate partisan manipulation.
  2. Neutral standards. In states that retain legislative control, stronger statutory criteria (such as compactness, respect for communities of interest, and explicit partisan-fairness tests) could help constrain abuse.
  3. Judicial oversight. In the absence of federal action, state courts and constitutions remain critical checks on gerrymandering. Several states have amended their constitutions to prohibit partisan maps, while courts in others have struck down extreme distortions. Ensuring judicial independence and preserving meaningful review will be vital to curbing excesses.
  4. Civic and legal tools. Advocacy groups can continue to use ballot initiatives, public map audits, and litigation under the Voting Rights Act to hold map-drawers accountable.

Restoring Trust in Representation

Ultimately, the goal of reform is to align congressional maps with the principle of fair representation. Whether driven by Republicans in Texas or Democrats in California, partisan gerrymandering undermines the core democratic idea that voters should choose their representatives, not the other way around.

Breaking the cycle will require coordinated action at both the federal and state levels. By enacting robust safeguards against partisan map-drawing, policymakers can help restore confidence that congressional elections are decided by voters and ideas, not by backroom negotiations with maps and markers.
