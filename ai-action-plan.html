Export, Regulate, Protect: The Tradeoffs in the U.S. AI Action Plan

Yunzhi Zhang · Aug 1, 2025 · 10 min read

On July 23, 2025, the White House unveiled America's AI Action Plan, accompanied by three executive orders implementing the plan. These actions frame AI development as critical to both the U.S. economy and national security. The plan charts a course for transformative, AI-driven initiatives: dismantling regulatory barriers, promoting open-source models, bolstering AI infrastructure, and expanding global exports of the AI stack.

Document Summary: America's AI Action Plan & Executive Orders

Pillar 1: Accelerate AI Innovation

  • Roll back overly burdensome regulation enacted under the 2023 AI executive order, reducing barriers to innovation.
  • Revise the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, removing references to themes such as "misinformation, Diversity, Equity, Inclusion (DEI), and climate change," so that AI systems are guided solely by objective truth and remain free of ideological bias.
  • As reaffirmed in the executive order on Preventing Woke AI in the Federal Government, AI model suppliers must certify that they have not intentionally embedded political or ideological bias; federal procurement contracts must include enforceable clauses ensuring adherence to standards of truthfulness and neutrality.
  • Promote open-weight AI models to stimulate innovation across startups and academia. Expand the National AI Research Resource (NAIRR) pilot to give researchers nationwide access to compute, data, tools, and training, ensuring equitable participation in AI research.
  • Accelerate AI deployment in key sectors, such as healthcare, defense, manufacturing, and agriculture, by establishing regulatory sandboxes and industry-specific AI standards.
  • Integrate AI education and upskilling into workforce development through vocational training, apprenticeships, and tax incentives, smoothing labor transitions so that AI augments rather than displaces human workers.
  • Deploy federal tools to support AI research, leveraging existing grant, loan, and R&D instruments to strengthen scientific AI advances.

Pillar 2: Build America's AI Infrastructure

  • Streamline NEPA review and invoke FAST-41 "fast-track" eligibility for AI infrastructure projects, including data centers, chip fabs, and energy facilities, to significantly shorten environmental review timelines.
  • As reinforced in the executive order on Accelerating Federal Permitting of Data Center Infrastructure, federal agencies are directed to deploy all available tools to fast-track permitting and construction of qualified projects.
  • Use federal lands to site AI-critical infrastructure such as data centers and energy facilities.
  • Leverage the leasing of suitable military bases to support mission-aligned data-center construction.
  • Mandate domestically manufactured hardware across the AI compute stack, from chips to software, free from foreign adversarial influence, and streamline semiconductor-manufacturing regulation to accelerate progress.
  • Upgrade the electric grid to meet AI's high power demands, enhancing grid capacity and integrating renewable energy.
  • Direct the Department of Commerce to offer financial incentives, including federal loans, grants, tax credits, and offtake agreements, to catalyze rapid investment in AI infrastructure projects.

Pillar 3: Lead in Global AI Diplomacy & Security

  • Promote AI exports: proactively export a full American AI technology stack, from semiconductors and cloud infrastructure to AI models, software, and vertical-specific applications, to reduce global reliance on adversary technologies.
  • As reinforced in the executive order on Promoting the Export of the American AI Technology Stack, direct the Commerce Department to bundle U.S.-made AI stack components (chips, cloud services, data pipelines, models, cybersecurity, and applications) for specific foreign markets.
  • Designate approved programs as "Priority AI Export Programs," giving them preferential access to federal export-financing tools.
  • Convene an international diplomatic task force to coordinate government finance mechanisms (direct loans, loan guarantees, equity investments, and insurance) to support these AI export initiatives.
  • The Secretary of State must develop a national strategy to promote U.S. AI technology exports and establish global AI standards aligned with American values and preferences.
  • Strengthen export controls on advanced AI chips and semiconductor equipment alongside allied nations to prevent adversarial access to cutting-edge technology.
  • Conduct rigorous national-security assessments of frontier AI models to ensure they pose no undue threats.

Issues That Remain

Several issues remain insufficiently addressed by the plan:

  1. Export ambitions vs. controls. The plan's push to export a "complete AI stack" is hard to square with current EAR rules and BIS licensing backlogs, especially alongside new restrictions on advanced chips. How the U.S. will balance ally access with technology protection is unclear.
  2. Sovereign-AI resistance. Many partners, including the EU and India, seek to build autonomous AI stacks to maintain strategic control. U.S. bundled exports may face reluctance in markets that prioritize local capability and governance.
  3. Regulatory divergence. The EU AI Act imposes strict general-purpose AI (GPAI) transparency and safety rules, effective August 2, 2025, with fines up to 7% of global turnover. U.S. deregulation-first policies may face compliance barriers or higher adaptation costs in Europe.
  4. Labor-transition gaps. Without targeted policy as a baseline to protect human jobs, AI adoption could outpace reskilling, causing job displacement and wage pressure. The plan's success therefore hinges on the speed and quality of training pathways, as well as on broader macroeconomic conditions that can generate enough new demand to offset dislocation.

Discussion and Policy Advice

I remain cautiously optimistic about AI's long-run benefits. AI will not eliminate the need for human labor, and many real-world problems remain ill-posed for current systems. But history suggests that major technological shifts involve transitional dislocation before net gains appear. Prioritizing innovation is defensible, but it must be matched with safeguards to protect people and sustain trust.

Three policy priorities stand out:

  1. Tiered deployment and infrastructure. Differentiate AI-stack exports by sector sensitivity. For highly regulated or strategic industries, deploy U.S. technical teams to deliver services and help build in-house models while keeping core technology protected; for lower-risk sectors, expand open-weight model availability to lower costs and spur adoption.
  2. Modernize baseline protections. Update consumer and youth-safety laws to set clear AI product standards without stifling responsible innovation.
  3. Support workers. Offer rapid training, wage insurance, and transition incentives to smooth adjustments.

AI's promise lies not in choosing between growth and protection, but in designing policy that balances the two, accepting that policy is about tradeoffs, while refusing to treat them as zero-sum.
